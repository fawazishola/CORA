# CORA Enclosure and 3D CAD

This document outlines the current direction for the CORA enclosure: how it should be modeled, which software to use, the dimensional thinking behind it, the material choices, and the practical risks that need to be tested before the enclosure can be trusted.

## Purpose

The enclosure is not just packaging. It is part of the product thesis.

For CORA, the enclosure has to do several jobs at once:

- physically protect the board and internals
- visually communicate the "transparent monolith" design language
- provide reliable mounting and alignment
- preserve airflow or thermal escape paths
- support maintainability during bring-up and debugging

## Recommended Software

The mechanical design work should be done in proper 3D CAD software rather than improvised in a 2D drawing workflow.

Recommended options:

- `Fusion 360`
  - good for fast concept modeling, assemblies, mounting studies, and enclosure iteration
- `FreeCAD`
  - strong open-source option if you want the workflow to stay fully local and accessible
- `SolidWorks`
  - strong if professional mechanical collaboration becomes part of the team

Recommended practical path right now:

1. use `Fusion 360` or `FreeCAD` for concept iteration
2. model the PCB as a reference body from the KiCad board outline
3. build the enclosure, standoffs, clearances, and fastening logic around the board
4. export dimensioned drawings for fabrication once the geometry is stable

## CAD Scope

The 3D CAD work should define:

- board footprint and keep-out zones
- enclosure outer dimensions
- panel thickness
- standoff placement
- connector access
- cable exit paths
- internal clearances above and below the board
- space for thermal management features

## Dimensional Direction

The enclosure direction described so far suggests a compact layered acrylic block rather than a large chassis.

The exact dimensions are still evolving, but the CAD model should explicitly account for:

- PCB length and width
- acrylic panel thickness
  - current visual direction suggests roughly `10 mm` to `15 mm` optical-grade acrylic
- standoff diameter and height
- connector protrusion depth
- minimum clearance above tall board components
- minimum clearance below the board for mounting and underside safety

At this stage, the most important thing is not pretending the final dimensions are locked. The important thing is that all dimensions become explicit in CAD rather than staying implicit in sketches or mental models.

## Materials

The enclosure materials are part of the product identity as well as the mechanical design.

### Primary Enclosure Material

`Optical-grade clear acrylic`

Why:

- supports the transparent monolith concept
- reveals the board and internal structure
- can be CNC machined or laser cut
- visually heavier and more intentional than thin consumer plastics when thickness is sufficient

Things to note:

- acrylic can crack if fastening or edge conditions are poorly designed
- thermal behavior is less forgiving than metal
- scratch resistance and edge finishing matter a lot for perceived quality

### Structural Hardware

`Stainless-steel standoffs and fasteners`

Why:

- visually reinforces the brutalist, structural design language
- gives the object a precision-instrument feel
- provides clear and serviceable mounting logic

### Board Finish Relationship

The enclosure material choice is meant to complement:

- matte-black PCB substrate
- exposed ENIG traces
- minimal visual clutter

The result should feel architectural and deliberate, not decorative.

## Heat and Thermal Management

This is one of the most important mechanical realities to take seriously.

Transparent enclosures can look excellent while still performing poorly thermally if the design treats the enclosure like a sealed visual box rather than a compute appliance.

Key thermal issues to pay attention to:

- heat generated by the SoC / FPGA region
- heat concentration near power-delivery components
- possible local hot spots around memory and video-output circuitry
- limited natural convection in a compact enclosed volume
- acrylic's poor role as a passive heat-spreading material compared with metal

### Thermal Design Considerations

The enclosure CAD should explicitly consider:

- vent placement or hidden airflow paths
- spacing above the primary heat-generating components
- whether passive heatsinks are needed on the SoC / FPGA region
- whether the enclosure can rely on passive airflow alone
- whether a silent active-cooling path is eventually required

### Important Practical Note

The enclosure should not be designed as if visual minimalism automatically wins over thermal reality.

If thermal testing shows the monolith form traps too much heat, the design should adapt. The product story is stronger when the object actually works than when it perfectly preserves an early concept image.

## Tests Still Needed

These are the key tests needed before the enclosure can be treated as trustworthy.

### Mechanical Fit Tests

- verify PCB-to-standoff alignment
- verify connector alignment with enclosure openings
- verify assembly tolerance stack-up
- verify no collision with tall components or cables

### Thermal Tests

- measure steady-state temperature under expected compute load
- identify hot spots on the SoC / FPGA region
- test passive enclosure configuration first
- test whether venting or heatsinks materially improve temperature behavior
- confirm the acrylic enclosure does not create unacceptable thermal trapping

### Serviceability Tests

- test whether the enclosure can be opened and reassembled cleanly
- test whether debug access remains practical during bring-up
- test whether cable connection and removal are awkward or risky

### Durability Tests

- assess cracking risk around mounting holes
- assess stress around fasteners
- assess edge finishing and scratch visibility
- assess stability when placed on a desk with attached cables

## Recommended Output From The CAD Phase

The enclosure/CAD phase should produce:

- a clean 3D assembly model
- dimensioned drawings
- panel-by-panel fabrication files
- standoff and fastener specification
- connector cutout specification
- a list of thermal assumptions to validate physically
